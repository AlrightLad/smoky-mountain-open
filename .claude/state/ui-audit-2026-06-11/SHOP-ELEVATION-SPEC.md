## Premium principles

- PREVIEW FIDELITY = SELLING FIDELITY. The store render must be the highest-effort render of the item in the whole app, not a shrunken thumbnail. Valorant builds its entire cosmetic strategy around the surface players stare at most (the gun) and shows it at full resolution with custom finishes; Fortnite skins read as 'premium' specifically because the shop textures, lighting, and effects are polished. WHY: a member decides to spend in the half-second they see the preview. If the preview under-sells the worn item, the item feels cheap and nobody buys; if it over-sells it, the worn version disappoints and trust dies. The preview and the equipped reality must be the SAME fidelity.
- SHOW IT WORN, NOT JUST SOLD. Every cosmetic needs an in-context 'on me' preview (mock avatar / mock card / mock nameplate) alongside the catalog tile, because the buyer is really purchasing a future version of themselves. WHY: virtual-store research is explicit that lifelike, interactive previews let buyers 'check features in detail' and make a confident decision; the value lives in imagining the item in use, not in an isolated icon. A cosmetic with no worn-preview is asking members to gamble ParCoins on faith.
- MATERIAL + LIGHTING DEPTH OVER FLAT PAINT. A 9.5 cosmetic reads instantly as a real material — it has a light source, an edge highlight, a shadow, a grain. The Founder's own bar names this directly ('real material/motion/depth ... not like flat paint'), and it's why a brass nameplate must look struck/beveled, not be a brass-colored rectangle. WHY: perceived value tracks craftsmanship cues. Luxury-packaging psychology shows presentation influences perceived value MORE than function; a flat fill signals 'free default,' while a lit, dimensional surface signals 'someone made this on purpose.'
- MOTION-ON-INTERACTION IS THE PROOF OF LIFE. Premium cosmetics react to the cursor/tap: a subtle tilt, a specular glint that sweeps across brass, a felt that catches light, a 200-320ms settle. WHY: 2026 micro-interaction research projects refined hover/press animation lifts conversion 15-25% and makes interfaces 'feel alive'; game inspect-screens add idle motion + audio specifically to turn a static image into tactile feedback. Static = a picture of an item. Motion-on-hover = an item you can almost hold, which is what justifies the spend.
- TIER THE CATALOG SO RARITY IS LEGIBLE AT A GLANCE. Establish 3-4 named tiers with escalating visual treatment and price, and make the tier readable from the tile alone (border weight, badge, frame). League's Epic > Legendary > Mythic > Prestige ladder works because each step is visibly, not just numerically, more. WHY: tiered scarcity is a documented driver — categorizing into tiers 'creates exclusivity as players are encouraged to purchase the more exclusive items.' A flat catalog where everything looks equal gives a member no reason to aspire upward; visible tiers create a ladder to climb.
- RESERVE THE TOP TIER FOR EARNED, NOT BOUGHT. The rarest items should be unlockable by tenure / achievement / 'being there,' never purely purchasable. League's Prestige skins 'can't be bought with RP' and require a currency you only get by playing; the loading-screen border 'signals you were there when the skin dropped.' WHY: the most prestigious signal is one money alone can't buy — it certifies status to other members, which is the entire point in a 20-person friend group where everyone sees everyone's profile. An all-cash top tier collapses into pay-to-flex and loses meaning.
- NAME + STORY EVERY ITEM — NO GENERIC LABELS. Each cosmetic gets a real name and a one-line provenance, not 'Gold Theme #3.' League's successful lines ('Dolmir's Revenge') win on theme and narrative; storytelling research shows narrative copy turns a product into a companion rather than a stale SKU. WHY: a name + story makes an item ownable and talkable ('I'm running the Champion Sunday set'). Members buy identity and conversation, not pixels; a story gives them both and makes the purchase feel like joining something.
- FEATURED + ROTATION FRAMING CREATES URGENCY AND CURATION. Run a small, curated 'Featured this week' slot that rotates, rather than dumping the full catalog flat. Valorant's Featured section rotates every two weeks; Fortnite's daily shop is built on this. WHY: NN/g confirms the scarcity principle makes people assign higher value to limited-availability things, and rotation provides built-in novelty so the store stays a destination worth re-visiting. A static, everything-always-available grid has no heartbeat and trains members to never check back.
- CONTROLLED DEBUT WINDOWS PROTECT EXCLUSIVITY. New top-tier items get a debut period where they are unavailable any other way; only after it ends do they democratize. Riot keeps new prestige content 'unavailable in loot pools until their debut period ends.' WHY: this rewards early adopters and gives the item a genuine 'launch moment,' so the people who got it first hold a distinction later buyers literally cannot obtain. It turns a purchase into an event and protects the premium-selling window.
- THE MOMENT OF ACQUISITION MUST BE A CHOREOGRAPHED REVEAL. Equipping/unlocking is a designed beat — a brief reveal animation, a confirming sound or haptic, a celebratory settle — not a silent state-flip. Luxury unboxing research shows a 'slow reveal or intricate opening sequence' magnifies value perception, and the experience defines value before the product is even seen. WHY: the dopamine of the buy is concentrated in the unlock moment; a cosmetic that just appears in a list robs the member of the payoff they paid for. This is where ParCoins convert to felt joy.
- REWARD EARLY OWNERS PERMANENTLY (TEMPORAL OWNERSHIP). When an item returns or a set expands, give original owners a distinct modified variant or a 'founding owner' mark. League gives first-purchasers 'exclusive modified versions ... in recognition of their investment.' WHY: in a tight, long-lived community this builds loyalty and a visible history — the people who supported the shop early carry a permanent badge of it. It also disarms the resentment of 'I bought it and now everyone has it' that otherwise poisons a small-group economy.
- BUNDLE MULTI-SURFACE PRESTIGE SO STATUS COMPOUNDS. The best cosmetics aren't a single asset — they touch several surfaces at once (nameplate + border + accent + icon) as a coherent themed set. League pairs skins with borders, icons, ward skins, emotes to create 'multi-layered status signaling.' WHY: a set that recolors a member's whole presence across the app reads far more prestigious than one isolated trinket, and gives committed collectors a reason to complete the set rather than buy one piece. Coherence across surfaces is the difference between 'a sticker' and 'a look.'
- THE SHOP ITSELF IS A PREMIUM SURFACE, NOT A TABLE. The store page must score the same 9.5 as the items — generous spacing, editorial hierarchy, a hero/featured slot, quiet confident framing. WHY: virtual-storefront research treats framing, focal length and curation as core levers; a cluttered grid signals a discount bin and devalues even great items, while a gallery-style layout signals that what's inside is worth money. Members infer item quality from store quality before they ever read a single tile.
- RESTRAINT IS THE LUXURY SIGNAL — SCARCITY OF EFFECTS, NOT JUST INVENTORY. Premium ≠ maximum bling. The most expensive-feeling cosmetics use ONE confident material moment, not five competing glows. WHY: peer brands (Stripe/Linear/Notion) and luxury goods both signal value through discipline and high contrast-of-restraint; piling on effects reads as a free mobile game, the exact opposite of prestige. One perfect brass glint beats a rainbow shimmer. This protects PARBAUGHS from looking like a generic game shop.
- DESIRE COMES FROM CONFIDENCE, NOT STATS — IT MUST FEEL LIKE 'THE BETTER VERSION OF ME.' Riot's stated design goal for evolving cosmetics is to make players 'feel confident and emboldened'; the benefit is psychological and superficial by design (and must stay non-functional to avoid pay-to-win). WHY: a member spends because the item makes their presence in the app feel more like who they want to be in the group, not because it does anything. Lean every cosmetic toward 'this makes me look like the kind of player I want to be seen as' — that's the actual product.
